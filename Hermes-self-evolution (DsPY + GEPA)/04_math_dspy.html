Part 1

What Even Is an LLM? (2 minutes)

Before anything else — let's make sure we understand what we're working with.

🔮
The World's Best Autocomplete

Your phone keyboard suggests the next word when you type. An LLM (Large Language Model) is the same idea, but it has read most of the text ever written by humans, so its suggestions are extraordinarily good.

It doesn't "think" or "understand" — it calculates: "given everything before this point, what text is most likely to come next?"

⚠️ The LLM has no idea what YOU want. It just produces the statistically likely continuation. To get useful output, you must craft careful instructions — that's the "prompt."

So What Is a Prompt?

A prompt is the text you give the LLM to steer what it generates. It's like the beginning of a story — the LLM continues it in the most plausible direction.

Vague prompt

"Translate this text: Bonjour"

Output: "Translate this text: Hello" — it continued the pattern, not your intent.

Clear prompt

"Translate the French word below into English. Return ONLY the English word, nothing else.\n\nFrench: Bonjour\nEnglish:"

Output: "Hello" ✓

Part 2

The Prompt Problem

Why can't you just write a good prompt and use it forever?

🗺️
Giving Directions to a Specific Person

You give your friend detailed directions to your house: "turn left after the big oak tree." Perfect for your friend who lives locally.

But if you give those same directions to someone from another country, they don't know your streets. The directions were calibrated for a specific person — they don't generalize.

Prompts are the same: a prompt carefully tuned for GPT-4 may completely fail on Claude or Llama, because each model has different "defaults" and expectations.

"Summarize this paper in 2 sentences. Be technical. Focus on methodology."

The problem: You now have to manually rewrite the prompt for every new model. As you build bigger systems with dozens of prompts, this becomes unmanageable. DSPy solves this.
Part 3

The DSPy Solution: Separate WHAT from HOW

This is the core insight. Everything else follows from it.

Key Concept: "Declarative"

When you say WHAT you want without specifying HOW to get there, that's called declarative. The opposite — specifying every step — is called imperative.

✅ Imperative vs Declarative — Click to Compare

You specify every instruction manually (the "how"):

# OLD WAY — you write every word of the prompt
prompt = """You are an expert AI assistant specialized in academic
research. When given a research question and supporting
context, you must provide a thorough, precise answer.
Structure your response as follows: first acknowledge
the question, then analyze the context, then provide
your answer. Cite all sources inline using [1] notation.
Use formal academic language. Do not exceed 200 words.

Question: {question}
Context: {context}

Answer:"""
❌ Problems: Breaks on new models. Hard to improve systematically. 20 hours of tuning for one task.

You specify only the contract — what goes in and what comes out (the "what"):

# DSPY WAY — you declare the task, not the prompt
class AnswerQuestion(dspy.Signature):
    """Answer the question using the context. Cite sources."""
    question: str = dspy.InputField()
    context:  str = dspy.InputField()
    answer:   str = dspy.OutputField()
✓ DSPy generates the actual prompt text automatically. Switch models? Re-optimize automatically. No manual rewriting.
Part 4 · Building Block #1

Signatures — The Job Description

A Signature tells DSPy what task it should do. Think of it as a job posting for an AI task.

Start with Math: What Is a Function?

In math, a function takes inputs and produces outputs:

f(x) = y
"Give function f an input x, and it produces output y"

A DSPy Signature is exactly this — but for AI tasks. It's a typed function declaration:

f( question: str, context: str ) ⟶ answer: str
"Give this AI task a question and context, get an answer back"
📋 Interactive: Anatomy of a Signature Click any part to see what it does
class WriteReview(dspy.Signature):
    "Write a 2-sentence review of the given paper."
    paper_abstract: str = dspy.InputField(desc="the paper's abstract")
    venue: str = dspy.InputField()
    review: str = dspy.OutputField()

What DSPy Does With a Signature

When you write a Signature, DSPy automatically constructs a prompt that looks something like this:

🔄 What DSPy Generates From Your Signature
Your code:

class WriteReview(dspy.Signature):
  "Write a 2-sentence review."
  paper: str = InputField()
  review: str = OutputField()
⟹
Auto-generated prompt:

Write a 2-sentence review.

---

Paper: [your input]

Review:

The optimizer can later rewrite this auto-generated prompt to perform better. You never have to touch it.

Jargon Buster: "InputField" and "OutputField"

InputField = data you provide to the AI. Like the ingredients in a recipe.

OutputField = what you want the AI to produce. Like the finished dish.

The desc= parameter is a hint to the AI about what should go in that field. More specific hints = better outputs.

Part 5 · Building Block #2

Modules — The Strategy

A Module takes your Signature and decides how to execute it. Same job description, different working style.

👨‍🍳
Same Recipe, Different Chef Techniques

A recipe (Signature) says: "inputs: flour, eggs, sugar → output: cake."
But how you make that cake depends on your technique:

  • Predict (beginner chef): Follow the recipe literally, answer immediately.
  • ChainOfThought (experienced chef): Think through the steps before acting.
  • ReAct (chef with a full kitchen): Reason + use tools (oven, mixer, etc.) in a loop.
⚙️ See the Modules in Action

Task: "What is 17 × 8?"

How it's called:

predictor = dspy.Predict("question -> answer")
result = predictor(question="17 × 8?")
What the LLM does:

Input: "17 × 8?"
Output: "142" ✗

It just guesses. No thinking. Often wrong on math.
Predict — When to use it

Simple tasks where the answer is obvious. Classification, extraction of structured data, simple lookups. Fast and cheap but no reasoning.

How it's called:

cot = dspy.ChainOfThought("question -> answer")
result = cot(question="17 × 8?")
What the LLM does:

Reasoning: "17 × 8 = 17 × (4+4) = 68 + 68 = 136"

Output: "136" ✓
ChainOfThought — When to use it

Any task requiring reasoning, multi-step logic, or analysis. The hidden reasoning step improves accuracy significantly. Use for math, argument analysis, complex Q&A.

How it's called:

react = dspy.ReAct("topic -> summary",
  tools=[search_arxiv])
result = react(topic="GEPA paper")
What the LLM does:

Thought: "I need to search for GEPA"
Action: search_arxiv("GEPA")
Observation: [results...]
Output: "GEPA is..."
ReAct — When to use it

Tasks requiring external tools: web search, database queries, API calls, file reading. The AI reasons about what tool to call, calls it, reads the result, and continues reasoning in a loop.

The Math of Chain of Thought

Here's something beautiful: Chain of Thought has an elegant mathematical explanation.

Without reasoning, the AI tries to go directly from input to output:

P( answer | question )
"Probability of getting the right answer, given only the question"

With Chain of Thought, we split the problem. We first generate a reasoning chain r, then generate the answer given that reasoning:

P( answer | question ) = Σr P( answer | r, question ) × P( r | question )
"Sum over all possible reasoning chains r:
probability of correct answer given reasoning × probability of that reasoning"
👀 Visualizing the CoT Math
🎲
WITHOUT CoT
P(answer | question)
Question → ? → Answer
The gap from question to answer is large. The LLM has to jump straight to the answer, often making errors.
vs
🪜
WITH CoT
P(answer|r, question) × P(r|question)
Question → 🧠 Reasoning → Answer
The reasoning chain r acts as a bridge. Smaller gaps = fewer mistakes.
Why this works: The LLM is better at "what's the next step in this reasoning?" than at "what's the final answer to this hard problem?". Chain of Thought breaks a hard jump into small, easy steps.
Part 6 · Building Block #3

Optimizers — The Automatic Improvement Machine

An Optimizer takes your program and makes it better — automatically, by searching for the best prompt.

🏔️
Climbing a Mountain in the Dark

Imagine you're on a mountain range at night, and you want to reach the highest peak. You can't see anything, but you can feel whether you're going uphill or downhill.

Optimization is the same: you can't "see" which prompt is best, but you can evaluate prompts (score them on test examples) and gradually climb toward better ones.

What Does "Optimize" Mean Formally?

Let's build the math notation symbol by symbol. Don't panic — each symbol has a simple meaning:

θ* = argmaxθ 𝔼(x,y)∼𝒟 [M(Pθ(x), y)]
θ=Our prompt settings (the instruction text + example demonstrations). This is what gets optimized.
θ*=The best prompt settings — what we're trying to find (the star means "optimal").
argmaxθ="Find the value of θ that makes everything after it as large as possible." (argmax = "the argument that maximizes")
𝔼[...]=Expected value = average. We average over all our test examples.
(x,y)∼𝒟=We're sampling input-output pairs (x,y) from our dataset 𝒟. x is a question, y is the expected answer.
M(a, b)=The metric function: how good was prediction a compared to the correct answer b? Returns a score 0.0–1.0.
Pθ(x)=Our AI program with settings θ, applied to input x. This produces a prediction.
In Plain English

"Find the prompt settings θ that, on average across all our test examples, make our AI program produce outputs that score highest according to our metric."

Why Can't We Use Gradients?

In neural network training, we use gradients — mathematical instructions for which direction to adjust each parameter to improve the score. This works beautifully for numbers.

Neural net weights (numbers)

Parameter: w = 0.7423
Score: 0.6
Gradient says: "increase w by 0.01"
New w: 0.7523 → score improves ✓

Works because numbers are continuous — small steps always make sense.

Prompt text (discrete)

Parameter: "Answer concisely."
Score: 0.6
Gradient says: "increase θ by 0.01"
New θ: "Answer concisely.???" ← meaningless ✗

Text is discrete — you can't "add 0.01" to a word. There's no gradient.

This is why DSPy uses discrete search instead of gradients: try variations, measure their quality, keep the best.

Part 7

The Compilation Algorithm

Here's exactly what happens when you call optimizer.compile() — step by step.

▶ Step Through the Compilation Process Click each step

Step 1: Bootstrap — Run on Training Examples

DSPy runs your unoptimized program on every training example you provided. It records the complete execution — every LLM call, every input, every output. This recording is called a trace.

Your 20 training examples: (question, expected_answer)

Example 1: Q="What is gradient descent?" → ✓ correct (score: 0.9)
Example 2: Q="Explain backpropagation?" → ✓ correct (score: 0.85)
Example 3: Q="What is a Transformer?" → ✗ too vague (score: 0.4)
Example 4: Q="Define attention mechanism?" → ✗ missing math (score: 0.35)
... (20 examples total)

The successful traces (examples 1, 2) become demonstrations — actual examples of good input/output pairs that can be injected into future prompts as "few-shot" examples.

Step 2: Generate Candidates

Now DSPy generates many candidate prompts to try. It does this in two ways:

Demo candidates

Mix different subsets of the successful traces from Step 1 as few-shot examples:

Candidate A: [example 1, 2]
Candidate B: [example 2, 5]
Candidate C: [example 1, 5, 7]
...

Instruction candidates

Ask a separate LLM to propose improved instruction variants:

"Answer the question."
"Answer precisely, citing sources."
"Provide a technical answer with math."
...

The search space is all combinations: (instruction variant) × (demo subset). Could be 100s of candidates.

Step 3: Evaluate All Candidates

Each candidate prompt is tested on a held-out validation set (examples the optimizer hasn't seen). Every candidate gets a score:

The validation set is important: we need to measure how well the prompt generalizes, not just how well it memorized the training examples.

Step 4: Save the Winner

The highest-scoring candidate is saved as a .json file. This file contains nothing but text — the winning instruction and the winning few-shot examples.

// optimized_qa.json (simplified)
{
  "modules": {
    "answer": {
      "signature": {
        "instruction": "Answer the question precisely and technically.
                          Include equations when relevant. Cite all sources as [1]."
      },
      "demos": [
        {"question": "What is gradient descent?",
         "answer": "Gradient descent minimizes f(θ) by updating θ ← θ - α∇f(θ)..."},
        {"question": "What is backpropagation?",
         "answer": "Backprop computes ∂L/∂w using the chain rule..."}
      ]
    }
  }
}
The Key Insight

This file is just text. Load it in milliseconds. No GPU, no model weights, no retraining. The "compilation" happened once; now you reap the benefits forever.

Part 8

How Few-Shot Learning Works

The simplest and most powerful trick in LLM optimization.

📚
Showing Examples vs Giving Instructions

Imagine teaching someone to write in a specific style. You could write a 2-page instruction manual. Or you could just show them 3 examples of the style you want.

LLMs learn much faster from examples than from instructions. This is called few-shot learning (learning from a few examples).

The math: including k demonstrations in the prompt changes the model's prediction from P(y|x) to P(y | x, demo₁, demo₂, ..., demok). The examples are "in-context" — they're literally in the prompt text, conditioning the LLM's output.

Check Yourself

Quick Questions

Q1: What is "declarative" programming?

Correct! Declarative = describe the goal. Imperative = describe the steps. DSPy is declarative: you say "I want (question, context) → answer" and DSPy figures out how to prompt the LLM to do that.

Q2: In the optimization equation θ* = argmax E[M(P_θ(x), y)], what does M represent?

Correct! M is your metric — the function that takes (prediction, expected_answer) and returns a score 0.0–1.0. DSPy finds the prompt θ* that maximizes this score on average across your dataset.

Q3: Why does Chain of Thought (CoT) outperform direct Predict on reasoning tasks?

Correct! Mathematically: CoT computes P(y|r,x)·P(r|x) summed over reasoning chains r. Each factor is easier to estimate than P(y|x) directly. The intermediate steps act as scaffolding.
